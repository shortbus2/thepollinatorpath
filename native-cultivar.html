Rabbit hole: plant labels

Native cultivars: native, but a little different

A cultivar is a selected or propagated form of a plant. Sometimes it is shorter, tidier, more colorful, or simply better behaved near a sidewalk.

Native plants wearing nice shoes

A native cultivar—sometimes called a “nativar”—comes from a native species but has been selected for a particular trait.

That does not automatically make it bad, fake, or ecologically useless. It also does not guarantee that every cultivar supports wildlife exactly like the straight species.

What can change?

Flower shape, bloom time, plant size, leaf color, sterility, and the amount or accessibility of nectar and pollen can all matter. A compact selection may be perfectly useful. A highly modified double flower may be the botanical equivalent of locking the restaurant doors.

I use nuance rather than purity tests: choose thoughtfully, observe what actually visits, and keep room for straight native species whenever possible.

Field note: It is okay for a garden to be ecologically valuable and still contain plants chosen because they make you ridiculously happy.

My shorthand

Native species: closest to the wild form.

Native cultivar: selected form of a native.

Non-native cultivar: useful sometimes, but not native to this ecosystem.
